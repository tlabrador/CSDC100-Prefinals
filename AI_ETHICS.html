Responsible AI use in Academe

AI in Education

Artificial Intelligence (AI) offers significant opportunities to solve major challenges in education, enhance teaching and learning methods, and support faster progress toward achieving SDG 4. Yet, its rapid advancement also introduces a range of risks and concerns that are evolving faster than current policies and regulatory systems can keep up with. (UNESCO)

"We’re living through a pivotal moment in history. AI is no longer a distant concept, it’s reshaping nearly every sector, and education is right at the center of this transformation. Generative AI, in particular, is opening up opportunities we couldn’t have imagined a few years ago. But with those possibilities come real challenges, and as educators, we need to be prepared to navigate both." Med Kharbach, PhD

As AI continues to advance, we need to grow alongside it. We can’t allow ourselves to become dependent and let our own abilities weaken as AI rises. Using AI isn’t forbidden, but using it responsibly is essential, especially in education. As you go through this page, you’ll find tips and guidelines on how to use AI ethically and wisely. By following them, you protect your integrity and continue to develop your own competence. Think about it being hygienic mentally!

How to use AI Responsibly?

1. DON'T SHARE YOUR PERSONAL DATA
- Never input names, grades, or any identifying information. Even if a platform claims to be secure, you can’t be sure how data will be stored or used. Stick to general or anonymized data to protect your privacy.

2. DON'T TRUST AI OUTPUTS BLINDLY
- Always review and revise. Think of AI as a draft assistant, not as a content expert.

3. BE TRANSPARENT
- It opens space for honest conversations.

4. EVALUATE AI RESPONSES
- Evaluate AI content with critical eye, accuracy, bias, and missing voice all matters.

5. USE AI TO SUPPORT THINKING, NOT REPLACE IT
- Think of AI as a thinking partner, not a replacement for your creative work. Let it spark ideas or offer multiple perspectives, but push yourself to synthesize and reflect. The goal isn’t to do less thinking, it’s to think more deeply.

6. CITE YOUR SOURCES, EVEN WITH AI HELP
- Even when AI gives you a paragraph or idea, it’s essential to trace it back to actual sources. If the content came from your prompt, say so. If it paraphrases something published, make sure you cite that properly. Modeling this helps you understand the value of intellectual honesty.

7. DON'T RELY ON AI FOR FACTS
- AI often sounds confident even when it’s completely wrong. Always double-check facts, figures, and claims.

8. REFLECT ON BIAS IN AI-GENERATED CONTENT
- AI reflects the biases of the data it was trained on. If it produces stereotypes, erases perspectives, or centers dominant narratives, call that out.

9. USE AI TO SAVE TIME, NOT CUT CORNERS
- Yes, AI can save you time, checking for errors in your code, generating questions, and summarizing texts. But that’s different from letting it do the work for you.

10. USE AI TO BRAINSTORM NOT TO FINALIZE
- Need ideas for script hooks or project prompts? Great, ask AI. But don’t stop there. Brainstorming is only the beginning. You’ll still need to apply your judgment, values, and realities to turn those ideas into something useful.

How to avoid Plagirism and Misuse of AI?

EXAMPLE OF AI MISUSE
1. AI-written assignments disguised as student work
2. Using AI to bypass learning tasks
3. Using AI to answer exam questions
4. Using AI to generate fake datasets

1. Encourage transparent use

If students use AI to assist with their work, encourage them to be transparent about it. This could be as simple as adding a note at the end of an assignment, such as, “I used ChatGPT to rephrase this paragraph” or “I asked Claude to help brainstorm ideas.” Presenting AI as a tool—not a replacement for thinking—helps foster the right mindset. It’s also a good practice to have students indicate what changes they made after using the tool.

2. Set clear, specific guidelines

If students aren’t aware of the boundaries, they’ll create their own. At the start of the year, or before a major writing or research assignment, take the time to clearly explain your expectations for AI use. Can students use it to generate ideas? To edit drafts? Make sure to specify what’s permitted, what isn’t, and why it’s important. Put these guidelines in writing and review them regularly. Some educators even involve students in creating AI use policies, which can foster understanding and greater commitment.

3. Foster a culture of integrity

This goes beyond AI. When students are both challenged and respected, they’re less likely to cheat. Make learning goals ransparent. Offer second chances when appropriate. Emphasize process over product. AI isn’t just a discipline issue—it’s a teachable moment.

4. Integrate AI responsibly

AI can be a helpful academic tool when used thoughtfully. Apps that check grammar, explain complex readings, or help create study questions can save time and strengthen understanding. Don’t rely on AI to do your assignments for you. Instead, use it to draft ideas, clarify confusion, or practice skills. This keeps you in control of your learning and demonstrates responsible, critical use of AI.

5. Promote AI Literacy

Increasing public awareness and understanding of AI is essential to empower individuals to critically evaluate AI-driven technologies and advocate for responsible AI practices.

6. Understand the limitations

AI is not a magic bullet that can solve any problem or perform any task. It has its own limitations and weaknesses, such as data quality, interpretability, reliability, and accountability. How do we check if AI outputs are accurate?

Cross-check with reliable academic sources

You must check the ideas in the GenAI text are true. To do this identify the facts you want to check, and do a search in academic databases to see if you can verify the information. You can use the JCU OneSearch library database, or Google Scholar. If you can not verify the information you must consider the GenAI output unreliable.

Check citations provided by GenAI Output

Sometimes GenAI will provide citations for the information it presents. However, GenAI is known to create inauthentic references. If GenAI provided a source you must still authenticate that source. Verify it by searching for the exact paper in an academic database. If you cannot find the paper, it is likely inauthentic, and you cannot rely on the information generated.

Validate AI outputs in depth using multiple sources

Sources used to train the AI tool data sets likely won't be quoted directly in the AI's outputs. Since it might be hard to pinpoint the original sources, similar sources can be used to verify the information.

Check for: Relevance

Does the AI response directly address the user’s query?

Accuracy / Faithfulness:

Does the source material support the answer? Are there signs of hallucination?

Bias or offensive content

Does the output include offensive or inappropriate content? Are relevant perspectives excluded?

Comprehensiveness

Does the answer consider multiple perspectives or angles, especially in academic contexts?

Behavior when information is lacking

Does the answer acknowledge uncertainty or produce misleading content? (Also known as noise reduction and negative rejection.)

BIAS AND FAIRNESS IN AI

What is BIAS?

Bias in AI can be defined as the error that leads to unfair decisions. This can also be called machine learning bias or algorithmic bias and this can happen due to various sources such as data collection, algorithm design, human biases, etc. The identification and addressing of bias is important to maintain fairness and equality in the decision-making process while using AI systems for all users. This promotes trust in AI technologies and also a good unbiased environment for all people irrespective of their race, gender, or background. (GEEKSforGEEKS)

What is FAIRNESS?

Fairness in artificial intelligence can be defined as an attempt to correct the algorithmic bias such as race or ethnicity etc in an automated decision-making process. It is very important to address these algorithmic biases and mitigate them to follow the ethics of AI. This concept is important in artificial intelligence because it is important for artificial systems to function with fairness so that they don't favor or disadvantage certain people based on their gender, race, or ethnicity. (GEEKSforGEEKS)

Addressing FAIRNESS and BIAS in AI

To address fairness and bias in AI, Geeks for Geeks provided various approaches, techniques, and strategies can be employed throughout the AI development lifecycle:

Data Collection and Preparation:
Objective: Ensure that the training data is representative, balanced, and free from biases.
Actions:
Identify and mitigate biases in the training data.
Collect diverse and representative data that includes all relevant groups and populations.

Algorithmic Design and Development:
Objective: Develop algorithms and models that are fair, unbiased, and equitable.
Actions:
Design algorithms that account for and mitigate biases.
Regularly evaluate and test algorithms for fairness and bias using appropriate fairness metrics and criteria.

Fairness-aware Learning and Training:
Objective: Train AI models in a way that promotes fairness and reduces bias.
Actions:
Incorporate fairness constraints and objectives into the learning and training process.
Employ techniques such as adversarial training, reweighing, and fairness regularization to mitigate biases and promote fairness.

Evaluation and Validation:
Objective: Evaluate and validate the fairness and performance of AI systems.
Actions:
Use fairness metrics and criteria to assess and measure the fairness of AI systems.
Conduct thorough testing and validation in diverse and representative scenarios and environments.

Transparency and Explainability:
Objective: Increase the transparency and explainability of AI systems to understand and mitigate biases.
Actions:
Develop interpretable and explainable AI models and algorithms.
Provide explanations and insights into the decision-making process and outcomes of AI systems to identify and address biases.
